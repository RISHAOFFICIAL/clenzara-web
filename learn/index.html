The Science of Parasitology

At Clenzara, we prioritize evidence-based gut health protocols. Parasitic infections are a significant clinical concern, and understanding the peer-reviewed evidence is the foundation of our "Science-First" approach.

Prevalence in Developed Countries

Research by Haque (2018), published in Antimicrobial Resistance and Infection Control, confirms that parasitic diseases are a global issue, not restricted to developing nations. The CDC currently monitors several "neglected parasitic infections" (NPIs) affecting millions of people within the United States.

The Biofilm Defense Mechanism

According to Macfarlane & Dillon (2011) in Nature Reviews Gastroenterology & Hepatology, biofilms act as a sophisticated physical shield for gut pathogens, rendering them up to 1,000 times more resistant to conventional treatments. Palestrant et al. (2004) established that these matrices are a primary mechanism for chronic gut infections.

Clinical Efficacy of Artemisinin

A comprehensive review by Hussain et al. (2020) in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology validated the potent anthelmintic (anti-parasitic) properties of Artemisinin and other phytochemicals found in Wormwood (Artemisia absinthium).

Black Walnut (Juglone) Bioactivity

Study results from Schmidt et al. (2016) in Parasitology Research demonstrated that Juglone, the key naphthoquinone in Black Walnut (Juglans nigra), exhibits significant inhibitory effects on the lifecycle of common intestinal pathogens.

Scientific References: Haque, R. (2018) PMC6013061; Macfarlane & Dillon (2011) Nature Reviews; Hussain et al. (2020) J Ethnopharmacol; Schmidt et al. (2016) Parasitol Res; Centers for Disease Control (CDC) - Neglected Parasitic Infections.
